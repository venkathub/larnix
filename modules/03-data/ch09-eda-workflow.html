The EDA workflow

Intermediate Browser Stable

The hook

A new dataset lands in your lap. Before you build anything with it — a chart for a report, a feature for a model, a claim in a meeting — you need to know what you are holding. How big is it? What is in each column? What is missing? What patterns are already there?

The trap is to poke at it at random: a .head() here, a guess there, a chart that looks nice but answers nothing. Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) is the cure. It is not a single trick — it is a short, repeatable recipe you run on every new table, in the same order, so you never forget a step and never start modelling on data you don’t understand.

Here is the whole recipe, and it is the spine of this chapter:

shape → types → missing → summaries → groupby → plot → questions

Each step is one or two lines of pandas you already met in this module. What’s new is the discipline: running them in order, and treating the output as evidence. By the end you’ll run the full pass on the Palmer Penguins dataset and answer three concrete questions — each backed by a number or a plot, never a hunch.

You’ll need from before: split-apply-combine with groupby (M3 Ch5) and reading a histogram, boxplot, or scatter (M3 Ch8). EDA is where those skills come together.

Step 1 — Shape: how big is it?

The first question about any table is the cheapest to answer: how many rows and columns? df.shape returns a (rows, columns) tuple. It costs nothing and tells you whether you have 300 records or 30 million — which changes everything about how you’ll work.

That returns (344, 8): 344 penguins, described by 8 columns. Small enough to explore comfortably in the browser, and the first fact in our running notebook of evidence.

Step 2 — Types: what is each column?

Next, ask what kind of value each column holds. df.dtypes lists the data type of every column. This catches a classic bug early: a column of numbers that pandas read as text (object) because one stray value had a comma or a stray space. You cannot average a column that is secretly a string.

Reading the list: the four body measurements and body_mass_g are numeric (float64 or int64), while species, island, and sex are object — text labels. That is exactly what we’d expect, so nothing to fix here. The point of the step is that you looked: numeric columns are numeric, categorical columns are text. If body_mass_g had shown up as object, that would be a red flag to chase down before going further.

Step 3 — Missing: where are the gaps?

Real data has holes. df.isna().sum() counts, per column, how many cells are empty. This single line tells you which columns are complete and which you’ll have to handle — by dropping rows, filling values, or noting the gap when you report a result.

Most columns are complete. The four body measurements each miss 2 values, and sex misses 11. So if you analyse sex, you are working with 333 known penguins, not 344 — a fact you’d want to state out loud rather than quietly average over. Knowing this before you compute anything stops a missing-value surprise from skewing a result later.

Step 4 — Summaries: what do the numbers look like?

Now describe the numbers. df.describe() reports the count, mean, standard deviation, min, max, and quartiles for every numeric column at once — a one-line statistical X-ray of the whole table.

Read down the body_mass_g column and you’ll find a mean near 4201.75 g and a range from about 2700 g to 6300 g — penguins averaging a little over four kilograms, with the lightest under three and the heaviest over six. The count row is worth a glance too: it reads 342 for the measurement columns, not 344, because describe() quietly skips the two missing masses we found in Step 3. The summary and the missing-value count agree, which is reassuring.

Step 5 — Groupby: does a category explain the numbers?

Summaries describe the table as a whole. The next question is whether a category splits those numbers into meaningful groups. That is exactly what groupby does: split the rows by a label, then aggregate each group. Here we ask whether average body mass differs by species.

The three group means tell a clear story: Adelie ≈ 3700.66 g, Chinstrap ≈ 3733.09 g, and Gentoo ≈ 5076.02 g. Adelie and Chinstrap sit within about 30 g of each other, while Gentoo penguins are roughly 1.36 kg heavier — a big, real gap. Species is not merely a label here; it explains a large chunk of the variation in body mass that describe() reported for the table as a whole.

Step 6 — Plot: can you see it?

A number convinces; a picture confirms and communicates. The last computational step turns the groupby result into a chart so the pattern is impossible to miss. A bar chart of the three group means is the natural choice.

The takeaway is one glance away: two short bars of nearly equal height (Adelie and Chinstrap) and one tall bar (Gentoo) standing well above them. The plot says the same thing as the three numbers from Step 5, but now anyone can read it in a second — which is the point of a chart in a report. The number is the proof; the plot is the explanation.

Step 7 — Questions: what can this data answer?

The first six steps are mechanical. The seventh is the reason for the other six: now that you understand the table, you can pose concrete questions and answer each with the evidence you’ve gathered. A good EDA question is specific and checkable — not “what’s interesting?” but “which species is heaviest, and by how much?” Here are three, each answered straight from the steps above.

Q1 — How complete is the data? From Step 1 and Step 3: there are 344 rows, and the only sizeable gap is sex, with 11 missing values. Any analysis of sex covers 333 birds, not all 344 — a caveat to carry forward.

Q2 — What does a typical penguin weigh? From Step 4: the mean body mass is ≈ 4201.75 g, with most birds falling between roughly 3000 g and 5500 g. “Typical” is about four kilograms.

Q3 — Does species explain body mass? From Steps 5 and 6: yes, strongly. The heaviest species is Gentoo at ≈ 5076.02 g, about 1.36 kg above Adelie (≈ 3700.66 g) and Chinstrap (≈ 3733.09 g), which are nearly identical to each other. The bar chart makes the split visible at a glance.

Notice that not one of those answers is a guess. Each points back to a specific line of output — a shape, a count, a mean, a chart. That is the whole habit EDA is training: every claim is backed by a number or a plot. When you later hand the data to a model, you’ll know what it can learn and what would mislead it — because you looked first.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  1. EDA is a repeatable recipe, run in order on every new table: shape → types → missing → summaries → groupby → plot → questions.
  2. The first four steps size up the data — df.shape, df.dtypes, df.isna().sum(), and df.describe() — before you compute anything fancy.
  3. groupby asks whether a category explains the numbers; a plot turns that answer into something anyone can read at a glance.
  4. Ask concrete, checkable questions, and back every answer with a number or a plot read straight from the data — never a hunch.

Exercise 1 — Guided (≈4 min) 🟢

Step 1 of the recipe is shape. Write eda_shape(data) that builds a DataFrame from a dict and returns its (rows, columns) as a plain tuple. Replace ____, then press Run.

If you’re stuck pd.DataFrame(data) builds the table; its .shape is already a (rows, columns) pair. Wrap that in tuple(...) so the return type is a plain tuple.
Show solution 
import pandas as pd

def eda_shape(data):
    return tuple(pd.DataFrame(data).shape)
pd.DataFrame(data) turns the dict into a table — keys become columns, so a dict with three keys of two values each is (2, 3). .shape reports (rows, columns), and tuple(...) returns it as a plain tuple.

Exercise 2 — Implement (≈8 min) 🟡

Step 4 is summaries. Write summary(data, col) that returns a small dict {"mean": .., "min": .., "max": ..} for one column — the mean rounded to 2 decimals, the min and max as plain ints. Complete the marked line.

If you’re stuck s = df[col] is the column as a Series. Build the dict with s.mean() wrapped in round(float(...), 2), and s.min() / s.max() each wrapped in int(...) so the types are plain Python numbers.
Show solution 
import pandas as pd

def summary(data, col):
    df = pd.DataFrame(data)
    s = df[col]
    return {"mean": round(float(s.mean()), 2), "min": int(s.min()), "max": int(s.max())}
df[col] selects the column as a Series; .mean(), .min(), and .max() are the summary statistics. Wrapping the mean in round(float(...), 2) and the extremes in int(...) returns plain, predictable Python types — so [1, 2, 3, 4] gives {"mean": 2.5, "min": 1, "max": 4}.

Exercise 3 — Stretch (optional, ≈8 min) 🔴

Open-ended, rubric-graded (not auto-checked). Load the penguins dataset and run the full EDA recipe — shape, types, missing, summary, groupby — then answer two concrete questions with numbers in your own print statements. For example: which species is heaviest, and by how much? How many rows are missing sex?

Rubric — a good answer:

  • Loads penguins with load_csv and runs at least three recipe steps (e.g. shape, isna().sum(), and a groupby).
  • Answers the heaviest-species question with a number (Gentoo at ≈ 5076 g), read from a groupby, not typed in by hand.
  • Answers a completeness question with a number (sex has 11 missing values), read from isna().sum().
If you’re stuck Run df.shape, df.isna().sum(), and df.groupby("species")["body_mass_g"].mean(). For the heaviest species, .idxmax() gives its name and .max() its mean. For the missing count, index the isna().sum() result with ["sex"].
Show one possible answer 
import pandas as pd
from lib.data import load_csv

df = load_csv("penguins")

# Recipe steps
print("shape:", df.shape)
missing = df.isna().sum()
print("missing sex:", int(missing["sex"]))
means = df.groupby("species")["body_mass_g"].mean()
print(means.round(2))

# Answers, each backed by a number
print("heaviest species:", means.idxmax(), "at", round(float(means.max()), 2), "g")
print("rows missing sex:", int(missing["sex"]))
This reports (344, 8), the heaviest species as Gentoo at 5076.02 g (about 1.36 kg above the other two), and 11 rows missing sex — every answer read straight from the data rather than typed in by hand.

Where this fits / what’s next

You now have a recipe you can run on any new table: size it up (shape, types, missing), summarise it, split it by a category, draw the pattern, and ask questions you can actually answer with evidence. That pass is the first thing a working data scientist does with a fresh dataset — and the habit of backing every claim with a number or a plot is what separates analysis from guesswork. This closes the data module; next you’ll carry these tools into the modelling work, where understanding the data first is what keeps a model honest.
