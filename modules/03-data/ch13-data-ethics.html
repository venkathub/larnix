Data ethics (a preview)

Beginner Browser Stable

The hook

You have spent this module learning to load, clean, and read a table. Before you build anything on top of one, there are three plain questions worth asking — and they take about a minute each:

  1. Where did this data come from, and may I use it? (provenance and license)
  2. Who is in it, and who is missing? (representation)
  3. Does it name real people? (privacy)

This is not a lecture on ethics. These are practical checks that save you from shipping a wrong conclusion, breaking a license, or leaking someone’s details. We’ll run each check against the penguins dataset you already know, then write the short “data note” that records the answers so the next person (often future-you) does not have to re-discover them.

You’ll need from before: the EDA recipe (M3 Ch9) — shape, types, missing values, and value_counts. The same tools answer these three questions.

Where the data came from (provenance)

Provenance is the story of a dataset: who collected it, from what, when, and how. The penguins table is body measurements of 344 penguins recorded at Palmer Station, Antarctica, by researcher Kristen Gorman and the Palmer LTER program. That single sentence already tells you a lot — these are real measurements of birds, taken by field scientists, in one place, in a few seasons. It is not a sample of penguins worldwide, and it was never meant to be.

Provenance matters because it bounds what the data can honestly tell you. A model trained on these three Antarctic species says nothing reliable about a penguin in a zoo, and a number computed here describes these birds in those seasons — not penguins in general. Knowing the source keeps you from over-claiming.

Why the license matters

A dataset’s license says what you are allowed to do with it — copy it, ship it inside a course, build a product on it, or nothing at all. The penguins data is released under CC0, which places it in the public domain: anyone may use it for any purpose, no permission or attribution required. That license is the reason this course can vendor the CSV — store a copy right next to the chapter — and why every exercise can load it offline at zero cost.

Swap in a dataset scraped from a website with no stated license, and none of that is safe. “I found it online” is not a license. Before you commit a file to a repo or train on it, find the license; if there isn’t one, treat the data as not yours to redistribute and look for a properly licensed alternative.

Representation: who is in the data, and who is thin

A dataset is a sample, and a sample can be lopsided. When one group has far fewer rows than another, every statement about that group rests on less evidence — the average is noisier, a rate could swing on a handful of records, and a model sees too few examples to learn the group well. Under-representation does not announce itself; you have to count.

Two cheap checks surface it: count the rows per group, and count the missing values. Let’s do both on penguins.

The counts are Adelie 152, Gentoo 124, Chinstrap 68. Chinstrap has fewer than half the rows of Adelie. That imbalance is not “wrong” — it is merely how the sampling fell — but it has a consequence: any claim about Chinstrap penguins stands on 68 records, so it is more fragile than the same claim about Adelie. If you later trained a classifier, this is exactly the kind of imbalance that makes a model quietly good at the common class and weak at the rare one.

Missing values are the other side of representation: a column can be present but empty for some rows, which thins out the group you can actually analyse.

Eleven rows have no recorded sex. If you set out to compare body mass by sex, those 11 penguins drop out of the comparison before you start — a small but real narrowing of the data. And the species shares (Adelie ≈ 44%, Gentoo ≈ 36%, Chinstrap ≈ 20%) put a number on the imbalance you saw above. Neither finding stops you from using the data; both change how strongly you should phrase a conclusion drawn from the thin parts of it.

🔬 The habit to keep: when a result depends on a small or patchy group, say so out loud — “based on 68 Chinstrap records” — instead of presenting it as if it rested on the full 344.

Privacy: don’t collect or print what you don’t need

PII — personally identifiable information — is any field that can single out a real person: a name, email, phone number, address, or government ID. The penguins data is comfortable to work with precisely because it has none: a penguin is not a person, the measurements were taken with research consent, and no row points back to an individual human. That is why it is safe to print every row in a public notebook.

Most datasets you meet later will not be so clean. The habits that keep you out of trouble are simple:

  • Don’t collect identifiers you don’t need. The best way to avoid leaking a phone number is to never store it.
  • Don’t print PII into notebook output, logs, or charts — that output gets committed to git, shared in a PR, and rendered on a public site.
  • Reduce before you share: aggregate (counts and averages reveal far less than raw rows) or drop and pseudonymise identifying columns.

A handy test: if this output leaked, could it embarrass or endanger anyone in the data? For penguins the answer is no. For a table of customers, it often is yes — and the fix is to compute on the data without putting the raw identifiers on screen.

Write a short data note

The cheapest way to make all of the above stick is a data note: three lines you write once, at the top of the analysis, recording the source, the license, and one honest caveat. It costs a minute and pays off every time someone (including you) reopens the work.

For penguins it reads:

Source: Palmer Station LTER / Kristen Gorman — body measurements of 344 penguins, three species, near Palmer Station, Antarctica. License: CC0 (public domain) — free to use and redistribute. Caveat: classes are imbalanced (Chinstrap only 68 rows) and 11 rows are missing sex, so conclusions about the smaller groups are weaker.

That note is not bureaucracy. It is the difference between an analysis a stranger can trust and a pile of numbers with no provenance.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  1. Provenance and license come first. Know where data came from and what you may do with it; penguins is CC0 (public domain), which is why this course can vendor it offline.
  2. Count before you conclude. Row counts and missing-value counts expose under-representation — penguins is imbalanced (Adelie 152, Gentoo 124, Chinstrap
    1. and 11 rows lack sex, so claims about the thin groups are weaker.
  3. Protect people. Don’t collect, print, or share PII you don’t need; penguins is safe to show because it names no person.
  4. Write a three-line data note — source, license, one caveat — so the next reader inherits the context instead of guessing.

Practice

Each exercise is auto-graded by run_tests, loaded for you from lib/grader.py. The first two use tiny hand-made numbers so the answers are exact.

Exercise 1 — Guided (≈4 min) 🟢

Representation starts with a share: what fraction of the whole is one group? Complete share(count, total) so it returns the proportion, rounded to two decimal places. Replace ____, then press Run.

If you’re stuck A share is one division: the group’s count divided by the total. Wrap it in round(..., 2) so the result has two decimal places.
Show solution 
def share(count, total):
    return round(count / total, 2)

run_tests([
    ("quarter", share(1, 4), 0.25),
    ("three quarters", share(3, 4), 0.75),
])
count / total is the proportion; round(..., 2) trims it to two decimals so 1 / 4 reads as a clean 0.25. This is the same calculation behind “Chinstrap is about 20% of the table”.

Exercise 2 — Implement (≈8 min) 🟡

Now do it for every group at once. Write class_shares(counts), which takes a dict of class → count and returns a dict of class → rounded share. Complete the marked lines.

If you’re stuck sum(counts.values()) gives the total across every class. Then build a new dict with a comprehension, dividing each value by that total and rounding to two decimals.
Show solution 
def class_shares(counts):
    total = sum(counts.values())
    return {k: round(v / total, 2) for k, v in counts.items()}

run_tests([
    ("imbalanced", class_shares({"a": 3, "b": 1}), {"a": 0.75, "b": 0.25}),
    ("even", class_shares({"x": 1, "y": 1}), {"x": 0.5, "y": 0.5}),
])
sum(counts.values()) is the denominator; the dict comprehension applies the same round(v / total, 2) to each class. Run it on {"Adelie": 152, "Gentoo": 124, "Chinstrap": 68} and you get the species shares from the chapter.

Exercise 3 — Stretch (optional, ≈8 min) 🔴

Open-ended, rubric-graded (not auto-checked). Load the penguins dataset and examine it for representation issues: count the rows missing sex (you’ll find 11) and the rows per species (the imbalance). Then write a three-line data note — source, license (CC0), and one bias caveat — as comments or print statements.

Rubric — a good answer:

  • Loads penguins and computes the evidence: missing-sex count and per-species counts read from the data, not typed in by hand.
  • Names the source and states the license as CC0 (public domain).
  • Records one honest caveat about bias — the class imbalance or the missing sex rows — and ties it to weaker conclusions for the thin groups.
If you’re stuck Use df["sex"].isna().sum() for the missing count and df["species"].value_counts() for the per-species rows. Then write three lines: where it came from, that it is CC0, and which group is too thin to lean on.
Show one possible answer 
from lib.data import load_csv
import pandas as pd

df = load_csv("penguins")

missing_sex = int(df["sex"].isna().sum())
counts = df["species"].value_counts()
print("rows missing sex:", missing_sex)
print(counts)

print("\n--- data note ---")
print("Source: Palmer Station LTER / Kristen Gorman — 344 penguins, 3 species.")
print("License: CC0 (public domain) — free to use and redistribute.")
print(f"Caveat: imbalanced (Chinstrap {int(counts['Chinstrap'])} rows) and "
      f"{missing_sex} rows miss sex, so conclusions about small groups are weaker.")
The note records exactly what a later reader needs: the provenance, the permissive CC0 license that makes the data safe to reuse, and one caveat that flags where the data is too thin to support a strong claim.
