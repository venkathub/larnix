Mean, variance & standard deviation

Beginner Browser Stable

The hook

Two cricket batters both average 40 runs an innings. One scores 38, 41, 39, 42 — clockwork. The other scores 5, 90, 8, 77 — thrilling and terrifying. Same average, completely different players. The average alone hides the story; you also need to know how much the numbers bounce around it.

That is the whole idea of this chapter. You will summarise any set of numbers with three values that work as a team: the mean (the centre), the variance (how spread out the numbers are), and the standard deviation (that same spread, back in the data’s own units so you can read it). Three numbers, and a list of any length has a story you can tell.

You’ll need from before: the idea of an average and reasoning about a set of outcomes (M2 Ch9 — Probability basics). That’s the only tool we carry in.

The mean: the centre of the data

The mean (the everyday “average”) is the balance point of a list. Add up the values and divide by how many there are:

mean = sum(values) / len(values)

For [2, 4, 6] that is (2 + 4 + 6) / 3 = 12 / 3 = 4.0. The mean answers “if I had to pick one number to stand in for the whole list, what would it be?” It is the centre our other two measures are taken relative to.

Variance: the average squared distance from the mean

Knowing the centre is not enough — our two batters share a centre. We want to measure how far the values sit from that centre, on average. Here is the recipe:

  1. Find the mean m.
  2. For each value v, take its distance from the mean, v - m (this is called the deviation).
  3. Square each deviation, (v - m)**2.
  4. Average those squared deviations.

That average is the variance:

variance = sum((v - m)**2 for v in values) / len(values)

Why square the deviations? Two reasons. First, deviations come in both signs — values below the mean give negative deviations, values above give positive ones — and if you averaged them raw they would cancel to zero every time. Squaring makes every term non-negative, so they add up instead of cancelling. Second, squaring punishes big misses more than small ones, so a wild outlier moves the variance a lot.

Work it through for [2, 4, 6], whose mean is 4.0:

variance = ((2-4)**2 + (4-4)**2 + (6-4)**2) / 3 = (4 + 0 + 4) / 3 = 8 / 3 ≈ 2.6667

And for [5, 5, 5] every value is the mean, so every deviation is zero and the variance is 0.0 — no spread at all. A variance of zero means the numbers are all identical.

Standard deviation: spread in the original units

Variance has one awkward feature: because we squared the deviations, its units are squared too. If the data is in runs, the variance is in “runs squared,” which is hard to picture. The fix is to undo the squaring at the end — take the square root:

std = sqrt(variance)

The standard deviation is the variance brought back into the data’s own units. For [2, 4, 6] it is sqrt(8/3) ≈ 1.633, a spread you can read directly: the values sit roughly 1.6 away from the centre of 4.0, give or take. For [5, 5, 5] it is sqrt(0) = 0.0. The standard deviation is the number you would actually report to someone, because it lives in the same units as the mean.

Note🧱 For Java developers

No special library needed — mean and variance are two short loops over a double[]:

double sum = 0.0;
for (double v : values) sum += v;
double mean = sum / values.length;

double sq = 0.0;
for (double v : values) sq += (v - mean) * (v - mean);
double variance = sq / values.length;        // population variance: divide by n
double std = Math.sqrt(variance);

One thing to know: there are two common variance formulas. We divide the squared deviations by n (the count) — that is the population variance, used when your list is the whole group you care about. Statistics libraries often default to the sample variance, which divides by n - 1 instead, to correct a bias when your list is only a sample drawn from a larger population. (In Python, statistics.pvariance uses n and statistics.variance uses n - 1; NumPy’s np.var defaults to n but takes a ddof argument.) Throughout this chapter we use the population version — divide by n — to keep the arithmetic clean. The n - 1 variant exists; you reach for it when you are sampling.

Let’s build it (worked example)

Let’s see what these three numbers describe. We plot a small list of values as points, draw a vertical line at the mean, and shade the band one standard deviation to each side of it. The mean marks the centre; the band shows the typical spread.

Read the picture: the dashed line sits at the mean, 6.0, the balance point of the dots. The shaded band reaches one standard deviation (about 2.83) to each side, so it covers the values close to the centre while the far dots at 2 and 10 poke out past it. A wider band would mean more spread; if every dot landed on the line, the band would shrink to nothing and the standard deviation would be 0.

Key Takeaways

Key Takeaways

  1. The mean is the centre of a list: sum(values) / len(values).
  2. The variance is how spread out the numbers are — the average of the squared distances from the mean: sum((v - mean)**2 for v in values) / len(values).
  3. Squaring stops positive and negative deviations from cancelling and weights big misses more heavily; a variance of 0 means every value equals the mean.
  4. The standard deviation is sqrt(variance) — the same spread brought back into the data’s own units, so it is the number you actually report.

Exercise 2 — Implement (≈8 min) 🟡

Now measure the spread. Write variance(values) — the average of the squared deviations from the mean. Compute the mean inside the function, then complete the marked line.

If you’re stuck Each value’s deviation from the mean is v - m. Square it with (v - m) ** 2, so the list comprehension reads [(v - m) ** 2 for v in values]. The function then averages those squares.
Show solution 
def variance(values):
    m = sum(values) / len(values)
    squared_deviations = [(v - m) ** 2 for v in values]
    return sum(squared_deviations) / len(values)
For [2, 4, 6] the mean is 4.0, the squared deviations are 4, 0, 4, and their average is 8 / 3 ≈ 2.667. For [5, 5, 5] every deviation is 0, so the variance is 0.0.

Exercise 3 — Stretch (optional, ≈8 min) 🔴

Open-ended, rubric-graded (not auto-checked). Take a small list of your own and compute its standard deviation — the square root of the variance — then explain in a sentence what that number tells you in the data’s own units.

Rubric — a good answer:

  • Computes the mean and variance of a small list, then takes the square root of the variance (variance ** 0.5 or math.sqrt(variance)).
  • Prints the standard deviation.
  • Explains it in plain words and in the data’s units — e.g. “the values sit about X away from the mean of Y, on average.”
Show one possible answer 
import math

def mean(values):
    return sum(values) / len(values)

def variance(values):
    m = mean(values)
    return sum((v - m) ** 2 for v in values) / len(values)

scores = [2, 4, 6]
m = mean(scores)
v = variance(scores)
std = math.sqrt(v)

print(f"mean = {m}, variance = {round(v, 4)}, std = {round(std, 4)}")
print(f"The scores sit about {round(std, 2)} away from the mean of {m}, on average.")
For [2, 4, 6] the standard deviation is sqrt(8/3) ≈ 1.63. Because it is in the same units as the scores themselves, you can read it directly: a typical score is about 1.6 away from the centre of 4.0. A larger standard deviation would mean the scores are more scattered.

Where this fits / what’s next

You can now boil any list of numbers down to three: the mean for its centre, the variance for how spread out it is, and the standard deviation for that spread in readable units. These show up everywhere in machine learning — for scaling features, spotting outliers, and describing how confident a model is.
